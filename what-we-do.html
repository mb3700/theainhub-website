The problem

Two problems, every time.

Every company we walk into has the same two: information stuck in silos and inboxes, and good people drowning in the busywork of moving it around. Underneath it, you know you need to start with AI—but not where, and you can’t bet the company on a guess. You don’t have to.

Where we go deepest
How we actually build it

The same method, every engagement.

There’s no mystery to it—it’s exactly how we work ourselves. We record an expert doing the real work, turn it into something reusable, build it into the tools your team already opens, then teach your people to run and extend it.

A human stays in the loop at every step. The goal isn’t to replace anyone’s judgment—it’s to take the busywork off their plate.

  • Record an expert doing the work (30–60 min screen‑share)
  • Turn it into a knowledge base + a reusable skill
  • Build it into Claude—and into internal apps with Replit / Vercel
  • Test, refine, and automate, with a human in the loop
  • Teach your people to run it—and extend it themselves
Proof

Three very different businesses. One engine.

Manufacturer, real‑estate firm, luxury homebuilder—same method, outcomes tailored to each one’s goal.

📡

Storm Smart

We turned the best rep’s hour of prep into an automated daily brief for every rep—CRM, county property data, owner research, and talking points. About an hour a day, back per rep. Then our analysis surfaced ~700 leads flagged for follow‑up that were never touched. Capacity and leakage.

🌐

IRC International Realty

We automated the tedious detail—shared‑drive hunting, taxes, investor reports—and taught the team to build their own new website and client portal in Claude Code and Replit. A tool they maintain themselves, freeing them to grow their client base.

🏗

A 35‑year luxury homebuilder

Great craft, 20‑year‑old systems. We mapped the shared drive and manual workloads and built internal apps—a construction‑site daily log and a customer‑service CRM—so the right information reaches the right person at the right time. They wanted a better‑run business, not more sales.
